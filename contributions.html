What I do

My research currently revolves around the understanding of mechanism at the individual level underlying macroecological and macroevolutionary patterns. Central aspects of my work are life history theory, physiology and the role of the light regime in governing foraging interactions and consequently species distributions. I work mostly, but not exclusively, on (meso-)pelagic fish, zooplankton and birds. For my PhD studies, I use mathematical models and dynamic optimization techniques to investigate key biological mechanisms that may prevent fish stocks from extending their range northwards. I enjoy thinking broadly, to link data with theory and models, and I find interest in diverse biological disciplines.
